Duality: The dual of the primal problem

Associated with any LP is another LP, called the dual. This relation is important because it gives us interesting economic insights. Knowledge of duality will also provide additional insights into Sensitivity analysis.

If we say the primal LP gives us the quantities of goods, we can think of the dual LP giving us the price of the goods.

Let's take a look at an example, given the following LP(primal LP):

        max \(16x_1 + 10x_2 \)

such that:

        \(2x_1 + 2x_2 \le 8 \)
        \(2x_1 + x_2 \le 6 \)
        \(x_1, x_2 \ge 0 \)

It's dual can be expressed as:

        min \(8y_1 + 6y_2 \)

such that:

        \(2y_1 + 2y_2 \ge 16 \)
        \(2y_1 + y_2 \ge 6 \)
        \(y_1, y_2 \ge 0 \)

Hence we have for,

Primal, max \(cx \)         =>     Dual, min \(yb \)
such that \(Ax \le b \)              such that \(yA \ge c \)
\(x \ge 0 \)                                 \(y \ge 0 \)


Or,

Primal, min \(cx \)         =>     Dual, max \(yb \)
such that \(Ax \ge b \)              such that \(yA \le c \)
\(x \ge 0 \)                                 \(y \ge 0 \)

Steps to follow

Obtaining a min Dual LP when given a Primal max LP

  • Change all the \( \ge \) constraints to \( \le \) constraints.
  • Create a variable for each constraint.
  • Get the transpose of A.
  • All variables with sign \( \ge 0 \) becomes \( \ge \) constraints, while urs variables become = constraints.
  • All constraints that are \( \le \), becomes \( \ge 0 \) sign variables, while those that are = become urs variables.

Obtaining a max Dual LP when given a Primal min LP

  • Change all the \( \le \) constraints to \( \ge \) constraints.
  • Create a variable for each constraint.
  • Get the transpose of A.
  • All variables with sign \( \ge 0 \) becomes \( \le \) constraints, while urs variables become = constraints.
  • All constraints that are \( \ge \), becomes \( \ge 0 \) sign variables, while those that are = become urs variables.

Important Theorems and Derivations

Note: P = primal problem, D = dual of the primal problem

(1) The dual of the dual is the primal.

(2) Let \(x\) be a solution of (P), a maximising problem, and let \(y\) be a solution for (D), a minimising problem, them \(cx \le yb\).

(3) If the primal objective function is unbounded, the dual doesn't have any feasible solutions.

(4) If the dual objective function is unbounded, the primal doesn't have any feasible solutions.

(5) If the primal doesn't have any feasible solutions, the dual doesn't have any feasible solutions or it is unbounded.

(6) If the dual doesn't have any feasible solutions, the primal doesn't have any feasible solutions or it is unbounded.

(7) Optimality in primal <=> in dual.

(8) If \(x\) is a solution for (P), \(y\) is solution for (D), and \(cx = yb\), then \(x\) is optimal solution for (P) and \(y\) is the optimal solution for (D).

(9) If (P) has a feasible solution and (D) has a feasible solution, then both the primal and dual have an optimal solution and their objective function values are the same.

(10) Complementary Slackness: Consider \((P^*)\) and \((D^*)\) in the standard form above. Let \((x^*, \mu^*)\) be a solution for \((P^*)\) and let \((y^*, \lambda^*)\) be a solution for \((D^*)\). Then \((x^*, \mu^*)\) is optimal for \(P^*\) and \((y^*, \lambda^*)\) is optimal for \(D^*\) provided that \(y^*\mu^* + x^*\lambda^* = 0\).

A popular way to think about Duality is that the Primal problem relates to maximising the number of items that can be packed into a knapsack, while the Dual relates to minimising the open space.
